The specialization preorder on $X$ corresponds to the natural order of ordinals
($\alpha\in\overline{\{\beta\}}\iff\alpha\le\beta$).
Since continuous functions preserve the specialization preorder,
any continuous $f:X\to X$ is monotone non-decreasing
($\alpha\le\beta\implies f(\alpha)\le f(\beta)$).

Now let $\alpha_0=0$ and $\alpha_n=f(\alpha_{n-1})$ for $n=1,2,\dots$.
This defines a non-decreasing sequence $\alpha_0\le \alpha_1\le \cdots$ in $\omega_1$.
Let $\alpha_\omega:=\sup_n \alpha_n\in X$.
For each $n$ we have $\alpha_n\le\alpha_\omega$, and hence $\alpha_{n+1}=f(\alpha_n)\le f(\alpha_\omega)$.
Consequently, $\alpha_\omega\le f(\alpha_\omega)$.
On the other hand, $\alpha_\omega$ belongs to the closure of $\{\alpha_n:n\in\omega\}$.
By continuity, $f(\alpha_\omega)$ belongs to the closure of $\{\alpha_{n+1}:n\in\omega\}$.
So $f(\alpha_\omega)$ cannot be strictly larger than $\alpha_\omega$.
This proves that $f(\alpha_\omega)=\alpha_\omega$.
